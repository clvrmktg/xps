Most shipments move smoothly when documents are correct and duties are paid on time.

But when something goes wrong—incorrect values, missing documents, or compliance issues—one question becomes very important:

**Who is responsible for the shipment?**

In customs terms, that responsibility sits with the **Importer of Record**.

## What is the Importer of Record?

The **Importer of Record (IOR)** is the individual or business legally responsible for a shipment entering The Bahamas.

This is the party that Customs recognizes as:

- The owner or consignee of the goods  
- The party responsible for the accuracy of the declaration  
- The entity liable for duties, VAT, and any applicable penalties  

Even when a broker prepares and submits the entry, the Importer of Record remains ultimately responsible for what is declared.

## How the Importer of Record is determined

In most cases, the Importer of Record is identified through the **consignee details** on the shipment documents.

These include:

- The commercial invoice  
- The Bill of Lading or Air Waybill  
- The Click2Clear registration  

If these documents are inconsistent—for example, different names across documents—it can delay the entry or create complications in processing.

For businesses, the Importer of Record is typically the registered company importing the goods. For individuals, it is the person named as the consignee.

## The role of the customs broker

A customs broker prepares and submits the entry on behalf of the Importer of Record.

This includes:

- Reviewing documents  
- Classifying goods  
- Determining value  
- Submitting the entry through Click2Clear  

However, the broker does not take ownership of the shipment.

The broker acts as an **agent**, not the responsible party.

This means that even when a broker handles the process, the Importer of Record is still accountable for:

- The accuracy of the information provided  
- The legitimacy of invoices and values  
- Compliance with import regulations  

## Why this matters

Many importers assume that once a broker is involved, responsibility shifts away from them.

It does not.

If there are issues with a shipment—such as:

- Undervaluation  
- Incorrect classification  
- Missing permits  
- Incomplete documentation  

Customs will hold the Importer of Record responsible.

This can result in delays, additional duties, penalties, or in some cases, seizure of goods.

## Common mistakes importers make

Even when a broker prepares the entry, the process depends on the information provided by the importer.

In practice, problems often arise from simple but avoidable issues in the documents and details supplied for the shipment:

- Using different names across invoices and shipping documents  
- Accepting pro forma invoices or quotes instead of final commercial invoices  
- Providing incomplete or unclear descriptions of goods  
- Failing to register properly in Click2Clear  
- Relying entirely on suppliers for documentation without verification  

These issues can slow down clearance or create compliance risks once the entry is submitted to Customs.

## Working effectively with your broker

A good customs broker helps reduce risk, but the process works best when the importer provides accurate and complete information from the start.

This includes:

- Ensuring invoices are final and detailed  
- Confirming consignee information matches across all documents  
- Providing any required permits or approvals in advance  
- Communicating clearly about the nature and use of the goods  

When the broker and importer are aligned, the entry process becomes straightforward.

## In simple terms

The Importer of Record is the party responsible for the shipment.

The broker prepares and submits the entry, but the importer is accountable for what is declared.

Understanding this distinction helps prevent delays and ensures that shipments move through Customs without unnecessary issues.
