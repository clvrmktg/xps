Mistakes can occur in any shipment.

An invoice may be incomplete, a value may be understated, or a classification may not fully reflect the goods being imported. In many cases, these issues are not identified until the entry is being prepared—or after it has already been submitted.

When that happens, the next step is a **post entry correction**.

## What is a post entry correction?

A post entry correction is a change made to a customs entry after it has been submitted to Customs.

This may involve updating:

- The value of the goods  
- The HS code classification  
- Freight or other charges  
- Consignee or importer details  
- Supporting documentation  

Corrections can be made before or after the shipment has been released, but the process and implications may differ.

## When corrections are needed

In practice, corrections are usually required when the information provided for the shipment does not fully align with what is declared in the entry.

This can happen for a number of reasons, including:

- Final invoices differing from initial documents  
- Missing freight charges at the time of submission  
- Incomplete or unclear product descriptions  
- Errors in classification  
- Discrepancies between documents  

Some of these issues originate before the entry is prepared, but only become apparent when the declaration is reviewed.

## Before release vs after release

The timing of a correction matters.

**Before duties and taxes are paid**, corrections are generally more straightforward. In many cases, the broker can submit a **Request for Amendment** through the system to update the entry.

Amendments may be required for issues such as:

- Incorrect HS code classification  
- Missing or updated documents  
- Incorrect declaration details (such as the wrong office)  

Corrections may also be required if Customs rejects the entry after reviewing or inspecting the shipment. This can occur where:

- The classification does not match the goods  
- Required documents are missing  
- Submitted documents are outdated (such as an expired business licence or tax compliance certificate)  

When the correct information is provided, these issues can usually be resolved without significant delay.

**After release**, corrections become more formal. Adjustments may require additional submissions, reassessment of duties and VAT, and in some cases, penalties depending on the nature of the issue.

## When and how corrections are made

The process for correcting an entry depends on its stage in the system.

**Before submission**, corrections can be made freely. The broker can update values, classifications, and documents before the entry is sent to Customs.

**After submission but before duties and taxes are paid**, corrections are made through a **Request for Amendment** in the Click2Clear system.

Amendments at this stage may be required where:

- The HS code is incorrect  
- Documents are missing or need to be updated  
- Declaration details are incorrect (such as the wrong office)  

Customs may also return an entry as **“Rejected for Amendment”** after review or inspection. This typically occurs where:

- The classification does not match the goods  
- Required documents are missing  
- Submitted documents are outdated (such as an expired business licence or tax compliance certificate)  

When the correct information is provided, these issues can usually be resolved without significant delay.

**After duties and taxes have been paid**, corrections are no longer handled through standard amendments. Further changes may require additional engagement with Customs, including the Post Clearance Audit process.

## What happens if corrections are not made

Uncorrected entries can create problems beyond the immediate shipment.

Customs may identify discrepancies during:

- Routine checks  
- Post-clearance audits  
- Future shipments  

This can lead to reassessments, penalties, or increased scrutiny on subsequent imports.

## The role of the Importer of Record

As with the original entry, the **[Importer of Record](/insights/importer-of-record-roles-risks-responsibilities/) remains responsible** for the accuracy of the information, even when a correction is required.

The broker prepares and submits the correction, but the responsibility for the declaration—and any resulting consequences—remains with the importer.

## In practice

Post entry corrections are a normal part of the import process. Not every issue results in penalties, and many corrections are resolved quickly when addressed early.

However, delays often occur when information is incomplete, inconsistent, or slow to be provided.

Accurate documentation from the beginning remains the most effective way to avoid corrections altogether.
