Clearing a shipment does not end the cost exposure.

Once a container leaves the port, a different timeline begins—one that is often overlooked.

If the container is not returned within the allowed period, **detention (or per diem) charges** begin to apply.

## What is container detention?

Container detention refers to charges applied by the shipping line when a container is held outside the port beyond the allowed free time.

After a container is released and collected, the importer is responsible for returning the empty container within a specified number of days.

If that deadline is missed, daily charges begin to accumulate.

## How detention differs from demurrage

Demurrage applies while the container is still at the port.

Detention applies after the container has been collected and is in the importer’s control.

The two are separate, but often occur back-to-back:

- Delays at the port → demurrage  
- Delays after collection → detention  

As outlined in [Demurrage, Storage, and Port Charges: What Delays Really Cost](/insights/demurrage-storage-and-port-charges/), costs increase the longer delays continue.

## When detention begins

Detention begins after:

1. The container is released from the port  
2. The free time for return has expired  

The allowed free time varies depending on the shipping line and agreement, but it is typically limited.

Once the period expires, charges apply on a daily basis until the container is returned.

## Why containers are not returned on time

In practice, detention charges usually result from delays after collection.

Common causes include:

- Delays in unloading cargo  
- Lack of coordination with trucking or equipment  
- Warehouse space not being ready  
- Partial unloading over several days  
- Misunderstanding of return deadlines  

In some cases, the container remains idle simply because return arrangements were not made in advance.

## The cost of detention

Detention charges are time-based and increase the longer the container is held.

What begins as a short delay can quickly become significant, especially where:

- Multiple containers are involved  
- Unloading takes longer than expected  
- Return scheduling is delayed  

These costs are separate from demurrage and can continue even after the cargo has been cleared and delivered.

## Managing container returns

Avoiding detention charges requires planning beyond customs clearance.

This includes:

- Scheduling unloading in advance  
- Ensuring adequate labor and equipment are available  
- Coordinating trucking for both delivery and return  
- Understanding the allowed free time for the container  
- Planning for prompt return once unloading is complete  

Clear communication between the importer, broker, and logistics providers is essential.

## The role of the importer

Once the container leaves the port, responsibility shifts.

The importer (or their logistics provider) is responsible for:

- Receiving the container  
- Unloading cargo in a timely manner  
- Returning the empty container within the allowed period  

Delays at this stage are not related to Customs, but they still result in additional cost.

## In simple terms

Clearing the shipment is only part of the process.

If the container is not returned on time, detention charges apply—even after everything else has been completed.

Managing the return of containers is just as important as clearing them.
