Not all customs brokerage services operate the same way.

In The Bahamas, a licensed customs broker is required to prepare and submit entries through the Click2Clear system. Importers cannot access the system directly to process formal entries on their own.

However, while a broker is required, the level of service provided can differ significantly.

In practice, this often comes down to the difference between **standard clearance** and **managed clearance**.

## The role of the customs broker

A customs broker is responsible for:

- Preparing and submitting entries through Click2Clear  
- Classifying goods  
- Determining customs value  
- Calculating duties and VAT  
- Facilitating communication with Customs  

This is the core function of brokerage.

How far the broker goes beyond this depends on the level of service.

## What is standard clearance?

Standard clearance is typically **transaction-based**.

The broker works with the documents provided and prepares the entry accordingly.

This generally includes:

- Receiving documents from the importer  
- Preparing the customs entry  
- Submitting the entry for processing  
- Advising on duties and VAT  

In this model, the process is largely **reactive**.

If documents are missing or incomplete, the broker may notify the importer—but does not actively coordinate beyond what is required to complete the entry.

## What is managed clearance?

Managed clearance is a more **hands-on and proactive approach** to customs brokerage.

Instead of only preparing and submitting the entry, the broker remains involved throughout the **entire clearance process**—from documentation before shipment to release at the port.

This includes:

- Reviewing documents before or during transit to identify issues early  
- Following up with suppliers or freight forwarders for missing information  
- Verifying that invoices, transport documents, and consignee details align  
- Identifying classification or valuation concerns before submission  
- Confirming that the importer is properly set up in Click2Clear  
- Coordinating timing to avoid delays at arrival  

In this model, the broker is not just submitting the entry—they are actively managing the conditions required for the shipment to be cleared without interruption.

Managed clearance focuses on coordination and compliance throughout the clearance process. It does not replace logistics services such as delivery, warehousing, or distribution, but it works alongside them to ensure the shipment can move as planned.

## Where the difference shows

The difference between standard and managed clearance is most visible when something is not in place.

For example:

- A shipment arrives without a Bill of Lading  
- Freight charges are not yet available  
- Documents do not match across the invoice and transport records  

Under a standard approach, the process pauses until the importer provides what is missing.

Under a managed approach, the broker may actively follow up, coordinate with third parties, and work to resolve the issue before it causes delays.

## In practice

A shipment arrives, but the broker cannot locate it in the system.

The importer has the commercial invoice, but no Bill of Lading. The supplier arranged the freight through a third party, and the transport document was never provided.

Without the Bill of Lading and the associated manifest reference, the shipment cannot be properly identified in the system, and the entry cannot be completed.

**Under a standard approach**, the process pauses until the importer is able to obtain the missing document.

**Under a managed approach**, the broker is already involved before arrival and can follow up with the supplier or freight forwarder, obtain the correct reference, and align the documents before the delay results in additional charges.

In many cases, this difference determines whether the shipment is cleared on time or begins to accumulate storage and demurrage charges.

In both cases, a broker is required to submit the entry.

The difference is how the situation is handled before it becomes a problem.

## Why this matters

As outlined in previous articles:

- Missing documents can delay entry preparation  
- Delays can lead to storage and demurrage charges  
- Incorrect information can require post entry corrections  

The level of coordination before submission often determines whether these issues arise.

## The role of the importer

Even with managed clearance, the importer remains responsible for:

- Providing accurate information  
- Ensuring documents are available  
- Authorizing the broker to act on their behalf  

The broker facilitates the process, but the accuracy of the declaration remains the importer’s responsibility.

For more on this, see [Importer of Record: Roles, Risks, and Responsibilities](/insights/importer-of-record/).

## In simple terms

A broker is required to submit entries through Click2Clear.

The difference is not whether a broker is used—but how the process is handled.

Standard clearance focuses on submitting the entry.

Managed clearance focuses on ensuring the shipment is ready to be cleared.
