Shipments rarely get delayed for no reason.

In most cases, delays happen because something is missing.

Before a customs entry can be prepared, the shipment must be properly documented and identifiable in the system. When key documents are unavailable or inconsistent, the process cannot move forward.

Two of the most common causes of delay are issues with the **Bill of Lading (or Air Waybill)** and the **freight invoice**.

## The role of the transport document

Every shipment must be tied to a transport document:

- **Bill of Lading (B/L)** for ocean shipments  
- **Air Waybill (AWB)** for air cargo  

This document confirms that the cargo has been received for shipment and provides the reference used to track it through the carrier and Customs systems.

Without it, the shipment is effectively untraceable.

The Bill of Lading or Air Waybill is also what links the shipment to the cargo manifest filed with Customs. Brokers rely on this reference to locate the shipment and begin preparing the entry.

If the document is missing, incorrect, or not yet issued, the process cannot proceed.

## When the shipment exists—but cannot be processed

A common situation is where the goods have arrived, but the documentation has not caught up.

For example:

- The supplier has issued an invoice, but the Bill of Lading has not been provided  
- The shipment was arranged through a third-party freight forwarder, and the consignee has no documentation  
- The Air Waybill number is unavailable or incorrect  
- The shipment appears on the manifest, but cannot be matched to the importer  

In these cases, the cargo may be physically present, but it cannot be located or processed in the system.

Until the correct transport document is available, the broker cannot proceed with the entry.

## The role of the freight invoice

In addition to the commercial invoice, the **freight invoice** is often required to complete the customs entry.

This is particularly important where freight charges are not included on the supplier’s invoice.

The freight invoice provides:

- The cost of transportation  
- The carrier or agent responsible for the shipment  
- Supporting details required for valuation  

Without this information, the broker may not be able to determine the correct value for VAT purposes.

As a result, the entry cannot be finalized.

## When freight details are missing

Delays related to freight invoices often occur when:

- Freight was arranged separately from the supplier  
- The importer does not know who the local agent is  
- Charges have not yet been finalized or issued  
- The freight forwarder has not released the invoice  

Even where all other documents are available, missing freight information can prevent the entry from being completed.

## Why brokers follow up for documents

From the importer’s perspective, it may seem like repeated requests for documents slow the process down.

In reality, those requests are what allow the process to move forward.

A broker cannot:

- Locate the shipment without a Bill of Lading or Air Waybill  
- Confirm the shipment details without matching documents  
- Complete valuation without freight information  
- Submit a complete and accurate entry  

Until these elements are in place, the entry cannot be prepared or submitted.

## The connection to delays and costs

Documentation issues are one of the most common causes of delay in customs clearance.

When documents are missing or inconsistent:

- Entries are delayed  
- Cargo remains at the port or terminal  
- Demurrage and storage charges may begin to accumulate  

As discussed in [Demurrage, Storage, and Port Charges: What Delays Really Cost](/insights/demurrage-storage-and-port-charges/), even short delays can result in additional costs.

## The role of the importer

While brokers prepare and submit entries, the responsibility for providing accurate and complete documentation ultimately rests with the importer.

This includes:

- Ensuring the supplier provides a final commercial invoice  
- Confirming that transport documents are issued and received  
- Identifying the freight forwarder or carrier handling the shipment  
- Providing all required documents to the broker in a timely manner  

Relying entirely on suppliers or third parties without verification can lead to delays.

## In simple terms

Most shipment delays are not caused by Customs.

They occur because the shipment cannot be properly identified, valued, or supported with the required documents.

When the Bill of Lading, freight invoice, and supporting documents are available and consistent, the entry process can proceed without interruption.
