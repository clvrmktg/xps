Most issues with imports do not begin at the port.

They begin before the shipment ever leaves the supplier.

By the time cargo arrives in The Bahamas, many of the outcomes—delays, additional costs, or smooth clearance—have already been determined by how the shipment was prepared.

Proper preparation is what allows the customs process to move without interruption.

## Before the shipment leaves the supplier

The foundation of every import is the **commercial invoice**.

This should be a final invoice—not a quote or pro forma—and it must clearly reflect:

- The correct buyer (importer)  
- A clear description of the goods  
- Quantities and values  
- Country of origin  

Generic descriptions such as “parts” or “supplies” can create problems later when classification is required.

At this stage, it is also important to ensure that the **consignee name matches exactly** across all documents. Differences between the invoice and shipping documents can delay the entry.

## Transport and documentation

Once the shipment is arranged, it must be supported by the appropriate transport document:

- **Bill of Lading (B/L)** for ocean shipments  
- **Air Waybill (AWB)** for air cargo  

This document links the shipment to the carrier and the cargo manifest.

The importer should ensure that:

- The transport document is issued  
- The reference number is available  
- The correct consignee is listed  

If freight is not included on the supplier’s invoice, a **freight invoice** will also be required to complete the customs entry.

Knowing who is handling the freight—carrier or forwarder—and who the local agent is can prevent delays later.

## Before arrival

Preparation does not end once the shipment is in transit.

Before the cargo arrives, the importer should ensure that:

- They are registered in the Click2Clear system  
- All documents have been provided to the broker  
- Any required permits are identified and in place  

Waiting until arrival to address these items can delay the preparation of the entry.

## At arrival

When the shipment arrives, the focus shifts to aligning the documents with the manifest and preparing the entry.

At this stage, the broker will:

- Locate the shipment using the manifest reference  
- Verify the documents  
- Prepare and submit the entry  

If the documents are complete and consistent, this process moves quickly.

If not, delays begin here.

## Release and collection

Even after the entry is approved, the shipment cannot be collected until both releases are in place:

- **Carrier release**, after freight and local charges are settled  
- **Customs release**, after duties and VAT are assessed and paid  

Delays at either stage can result in storage, demurrage, or detention charges.

## The key principle

Most delays are not caused by Customs.

They are the result of missing, inconsistent, or late information provided before or during shipment.

When documentation is complete and aligned from the beginning, the clearance process is usually straightforward.

## In simple terms

A well-prepared shipment:

1. Has complete and consistent documents  
2. Can be identified in the system  
3. Can be valued and classified correctly  
4. Can be processed without delay  

Preparation reduces the need for corrections, avoids unnecessary costs, and allows cargo to move as expected.
