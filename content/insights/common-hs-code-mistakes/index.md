Not all import mistakes cause delays.

Some go through without issue—but result in the wrong duty being paid.

One of the most common causes of this is incorrect **HS code classification**.

## What is an HS code?

An HS (Harmonized System) code is used to classify goods for import.

Each product is assigned a code that determines:

- The applicable duty rate  
- Whether VAT applies  
- Whether permits or restrictions are required  

Accurate classification is essential to ensure the correct assessment of duties and taxes.

## Why classification matters

In The Bahamas, duty rates can vary significantly depending on how a product is classified.

Two items that appear similar may fall under different codes with different rates.

For example, small differences in:

- Material  
- Function  
- Intended use  

can result in a different classification—and a different duty rate.

## Common classification mistakes

In practice, classification errors often come from how goods are described or understood.

Some of the most common issues include:

### Using broad or generic descriptions

Descriptions such as:

- “Parts”  
- “Accessories”  
- “Supplies”  

do not provide enough detail for accurate classification.

This can lead to incorrect or inconsistent code selection.

### Relying on supplier descriptions

Suppliers often describe goods for commercial purposes, not for customs classification.

An invoice description may not reflect how the item should be classified under the tariff.

### Grouping different items under one code

Where shipments contain multiple types of goods, each item may require its own classification.

Combining them under a single code can result in incorrect duty being applied.

### Misidentifying the function of the product

Classification is based primarily on what a product **is and does**.

Misunderstanding the function can lead to selecting the wrong code.

### Choosing codes based on duty rate

In some cases, codes are selected based on perceived advantage rather than accuracy.

This creates risk if the classification does not match the actual goods.

## The cost of getting it wrong

Incorrect classification can result in:

- **Overpayment of duty**  
- **Underpayment of duty**  
- **Delays if flagged during review or inspection**  
- **Corrections after submission**  
- **Additional scrutiny on future shipments**  

Where duty is underpaid, the importer may be required to settle the difference after reassessment.

Where duty is overpaid, recovery may require additional steps and is not always immediate.

As outlined in  
[Post Entry Corrections: Fixing Mistakes Without Delays](/insights/post-entry-corrections/),  
changes after submission can become more complex depending on the stage of the entry.

## When mistakes are identified

Classification issues may be identified:

- During entry preparation  
- During Customs review  
- At the time of inspection  
- After release, through audit or review  

When identified early, corrections are usually straightforward.

When identified later, they may involve reassessment, delays, or additional documentation.

## The role of the importer and broker

The importer is responsible for the accuracy of the declaration, including classification.

The broker applies classification based on the information provided and their understanding of the goods.

Accurate classification depends on:

- Clear and complete product descriptions  
- Supporting information where needed  
- Consistency across documents  

## In simple terms

Classification determines how much you pay.

When it is correct, duties and VAT are assessed properly and the process moves smoothly.

When it is incorrect, the cost may not be immediately visible—but it will eventually need to be addressed.
